## Issue 1: Voicemail Scene Patch

**Current problem:** The voicemail is played/written as emotionally heightened —
a moment of urgency, pleading, or tenderness that signals Will knows something
important is happening.

**Why this is structurally wrong:**
If Will's tone is heightened, the voicemail becomes Will's emotional arc — EMIT frame.
The audience reads: "Will tried. He showed his love. He was too late."
That is an Ordeal Arc reading (ARC-001) — Will as a person who attempted R1.
But the structural reality is Type B Closure (ARC-002) — R1 was unavailable.
The tone of the message must encode that Will did NOT know he was in the arc.

**V's production note (adopted verbatim):**
> "The voicemail must not be performed as emotional confession.
> It must be performed as ordinary signal that becomes evidence.
> The horror is its ordinariness."

**Scene direction patch:**

```
[CURRENT — wrong]:
Will's voice on the voicemail is slightly strained, carries weight.
The audience understands he senses something.

[REVISED — structurally accurate]:
Will's voice on the voicemail is unremarkable.
He sounds like someone leaving a Tuesday message.
"Hey, it's Dad. Just checking in. Call me when you get a chance."
Maybe a small specific detail — something concrete, not emotional.
The voicemail machine beeps.
That's all.

The audience hears: an ordinary man who did not know.
The record machine heard something else.
```

**What the voicemail captures structurally:**
- Will's ELEOS functional state at the moment of prevented contact
- Not his love (subjective) but his operational signature (structural)
- The gap between what he was doing (routine contact attempt) and
  what was happening (the arc terminating without him)

## Issue 2: Love's Act III Placement

**Current problem:** Love's Act III placement undermines "record as antagonist" logic.

**Structural diagnosis:**
If Will's love is demonstrated in Act III, the audience reads the arc as:
"Will loved her; he was too late; the love is the tragedy."
This is an EMIT/Ordeal reading — the love as the thing that almost saved her.
The record-as-antagonist logic requires that **the record is the structuring agent
of the arc, not Will's love.**

**The structural function of the record:**
The record (delay pattern) doesn't care about Will's love.
The record is a mechanical pattern — a feedback loop (L2) in Will's behavior
that was in place before the crisis, that accumulated consequences
without Will's awareness, and that terminated the arc without his consent.

Love is not the record's antagonist. The record is.
The record is not evil. It is structural.
The tragedy is that structural patterns don't negotiate with love.

**Scene placement patch:**

```
Love is not Act III material.
Love is the presence that makes the record's operation visible.

The record-as-antagonist logic requires:
  Act I: The record establishes itself as a pattern (not dramatic, not emotional — mechanical)
  Act II: The record accumulates — Will's delay operates as the feedback loop
          that makes the arc possible
  Act III: The record delivers its evidence

Love should be present throughout as background — 
not the subject, but the reason the structural gap matters.
Love is the context that gives the record its weight.
The record is the antagonist because it operated without caring about the love.

If love arrives as an Act III revelation, it invites the Ordeal Arc misread:
"If only he'd loved harder, sooner, more visibly."
That is WEAP-001 territory — demanding the person in T3 demonstrate R1.

Love is the given. The record is the structural agent.
```
