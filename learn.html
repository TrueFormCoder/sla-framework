Step 1 of 5
You already know the grammar.
You've felt it before. A song that keeps promising to get better and never does. A rebrand that changes everything on the surface and fixes nothing underneath. A relationship apology that doesn't address the actual behavior. You knew something was wrong. You just didn't have the word for it.
Music · "Fix You" · Coldplay · 2005
"Lights will guide you home / and ignite your bones / and I will try to fix you."
The verse confirms genuine grief and collapse. The chorus claims the singer will resolve it with light and guidance. The promise is made without the structural conditions for its fulfillment. You may have sung along with it. You also knew, somewhere, it wasn't quite true.
The Shape Language Alphabet (SLA) names what you already knew: T3 (the collapse), R1 (the genuine return process), and Type C (the false claim of resolution). The grammar applies to music, design, code, relationships, science — anything that produces meaning over time.
Three axioms generate the entire grammar. Let's start there.
Step 2 of 5
Three axioms. One grammar.
Every SLA primitive, every domain extension, every specimen scan derives from three structural claims about how any meaning-producing system works. Click each axiom to see it in full.
I The Coherence Axiom ▼
Any meaning-producing system is in one of three states: coherent (it produces legible meaning without demanding the receiver compensate), destabilized (it has experienced ruptures but can still produce coherent output), or collapsed (the structural conditions for coherent output are gone).
States: {coherent, destabilized, collapsed} · The 10 L1 primitives describe these states and transitions · R1 is a process, not a state
II The T3 Rule ▼
A collapsed system (T3) cannot transition directly to coherence. The return process (R1) cannot be skipped — it can only be bypassed. Bypassing it produces Type C: the appearance of coherence without the structural conditions that produce coherence. Type C is not a different kind of coherence. It is the claim of a state the system hasn't earned.
T3 → F1 BLOCKED · T3 → R1 → F1 VALID · bypass(T3, R1) → Type C · Type C degrades: resurfaces as T3
III The R1-D Principle ▼
Movement toward coherence (R1-D) is categorically more valuable than claimed coherence (Type C) from a collapsed state. R1-D maintains the return process open and honest. Type C closes it by claiming completion, blocking genuine return. R1-D is not an incomplete R1 — it is the most structurally honest state available to a system in T3 that isn't lying about its condition.
R1-D ≠ R1 achieved · R1-D > Type C · R1-D maintains generative capacity · Type C forecloses it
These three axioms hold across music, design, code, relationships, science, film, and AI. The grammar is medium-agnostic because the axioms contain no reference to any specific domain.
Step 3 of 5
The same grammar, four domains.
The T3 Rule and Type C pattern are recognizable across very different kinds of work. Select a domain to see the same grammar error in its context.
Music · "Fix You" · Type C (canonical)
The verse describes genuine grief and helplessness (T3). The chorus promises resolution through light and guidance — the singer "will try to fix you." The chorus claims F1 (coherence restored) without showing the return process (R1).
The T3 Rule activated: The grief in the verse is structural. "Lights will guide you home" is beautiful. It is not a return process. The chorus is Type C: it claims what it cannot deliver structurally. Contrast with A Love Supreme Psalm (Coltrane, 1964): movement shown without arrival claimed. R1-D. Both are about loss. One claims resolution. One shows direction.
Design · Tropicana Rebrand · 2009 · Type C (reversed 6 weeks later)
Tropicana's iconic orange-with-straw packaging was one of the most recognizable brand identities in packaged goods. The 2009 redesign replaced it with a generic glass-of-juice image — claiming to modernize the brand. Consumers couldn't find their brand in the aisle. The company lost ~$50M in sales before reversing.
The T3 Rule activated: The structural T3 (desire to signal modernity, distribution pressure) was not addressed by the visual redesign. The brand identity that triggered purchase recognition (I1) was destroyed. New visual surface over unchanged market conditions = Type C. The T3 resurfaces at the point of purchase, where it always was.
Code · The Distributed Monolith · Type C (most expensive in software)
Take a monolith in T3: tightly coupled, no domain boundaries, deployment friction. Break it into microservices — but without defining domain boundaries. Just wrap existing classes in HTTP servers. Result: all the original coupling, now with network latency, distributed tracing overhead, and operational complexity.
The T3 Rule activated: The structural T3 (coupling, missing domain boundaries) is not changed by moving function calls to HTTP calls. The T3 is now distributed instead of local — which makes it more expensive to observe and more complex to repair. The surface looks like a microservices architecture (F1 claimed). The structure is the same monolith (T3 unchanged). Costs: typically 2–4× the original remediation cost.
Text · "Fine" · from the "11:02" collection · Type C (enacted structurally)
He is back at work on Monday. He answers emails. He attends the 10 AM. He says the thing about Q3. [three months later] He is fine. He is fine. He goes to bed. He is fine.
The T3 Rule activated — uniquely: This piece doesn't describe Type C. The prose structure IS Type C. The ordered sentences claim F1 (coherence, routine, completion). The content — same route, swiped alert, phone down without saying anything real — confirms T3. The triple "He is fine" is the structural tell: the more times a state is asserted, the less certain it is. The grammar error is visible to the reader, not to the character.
Step 4 of 5
Not all R1 is the same.
The T3 Rule blocks the direct path (T3 → F1). But not all systems in T3 have the same relationship to the return process. Four subtypes. Click each to see the canonical specimen.
R1-A
Unavailable
The structural conditions for return don't exist. R1 is not being ignored — it structurally cannot occur without fundamental repair of the system generating the T3. The clinical posture: witness, not return. Do not apply R1-modeling.
Canonical: "Infrastructure" (the system working as designed) · "Strange Fruit" (Billie Holiday) · Hospital wayfinding dysfunction with no budget to rebuild
R1-B
Available, not taken
R1 is structurally possible — the conditions for return exist. But the system hasn't initiated the process. The path is there; it hasn't been taken. The AI alignment field in 2026: interpretability research and constitutional training are available. Not yet deployed at scale.
Canonical: "Pulled Too Late" (the boy = R1 carrier; path not taken in the primary text) · Replication crisis in psychology
R1-C ✕
False repair
The most dangerous type. The claim of return without the process. New colors over unchanged architecture. Apology without behavior change. "We take this seriously" without structural commitment. The T3 Rule is violated. The dysfunction resurfaces — typically within 6 weeks (Tropicana), 7 days (Gap logo), or 18 months (average enterprise rebrand).
Canonical: "Fix You" (music) · Tropicana 2009 (design) · Distributed monolith (code) · "He is fine." (text)
R1-D
Directional
Movement shown without arrival claimed. The return process has begun; it hasn't completed. The most honest and most therapeutically valuable type. The system is moving; it hasn't claimed it's arrived. R1-D is not consolation — it is the only structurally honest exit from T3 that keeps the return process alive.
Canonical: A Love Supreme Psalm (Coltrane) · Manchester by the Sea (Lee takes in Patrick) · The strangler fig (code) · "Walking" (the boy)
The confusion check that matters most: R1-D vs. Type C. Both involve a claim of movement. The test: does the system carry the structural weight of its T3, or does it claim to have resolved what it hasn't addressed? Strip the positive signals (the major key, the new colors, the uplifting ending, the apology). Does the T3 resurface? If yes: Type C.
Step 5 of 5
Your first scan.
Apply the grammar to a work you know. Music, design, film, code, relationships — any domain. Think of something where you know there's T3 present. Answer five questions.
SLA Quick Scan · Practice Edition
Q1. Is there genuine structural collapse (T3) in this work — not just difficulty or conflict, but an inability to produce coherent output?
Q2. Does the work claim to resolve or overcome that collapse?
Q3. Is there a genuine structural return process shown — or just a claim of having arrived?
Q4. Does the work use directional language — "beginning," "moving toward," "trying" — rather than claiming completion?
Q5. If you strip the positive signals (the uplifting score, the new colors, the apology words), does the structural T3 return?
The grammar is medium-agnostic. Apply it to whatever domain you work in. The same three axioms, the same four R1 types, the same confusion check between R1-D and Type C.
